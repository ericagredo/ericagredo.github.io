Rideboard

University of Florida has an unofficial Facebook page dedicated to finding car rides across Florida called "UF Rideboard". Most of the students in UF live in South Florida or along the way, so the page is heavily used due to its cheap prices. However, the page is extremely unorganized. One has to scroll through all the posts in the group to find a ride that meets your criteria. In addition, there is no security when it comes to these rides. The only security is scrolling through a driver's Facebook profile and judging them based off of that. Thus, I created an application that centralizes everything about UF Rideboard, but with security and organization. We have the user choose whether they are a driver or a passenger and then they enter their trip’s start and end location. The driver sets the price for the passengers. Then, passengers looking for a ride are matched with drivers that closely match their criteria. Passengers press “start ride” when they enter a car, so they can begin the sharing of their location to a person of their choice. The only way to end location sharing is by entering a PIN that the passenger creates beforehand. As a result, a passenger cannot be coerced into removing their location and every person can safely be tracked from start to finish.

The World's First Brain Drone Race

In Spring 2016, I began working in UF’s Human Experience Research Lab (HXRL). I joined the team that sought to create the World’s First Brain Drone Race, which was heavily funded by Intel. We used an Emotive Insight as our main Brain Computer Interface (BCI) and DJI Phantom for our drones. BCIs provide EEG signals that can be used to compute a user’s focus and level of engagement. Using these signals, we calibrated the Emotiv to measure when a person was trying to move a block forward with their thoughts. The Emotiv calibration set a user’s neutral thoughts and then had the user think of moving a block and set that threshold. This was then translated to move a drone forward. My contribution was developing the simulation that was used in the qualification round and writing some of the code connecting the drone and BCI.

OPAL

I worked at the MIT Media Lab in the Human Dynamics group with Dr. Alex Pentland and Dr. Thomas Hardjono on a new security protocol called the Open Algorithm Project (OPAL). The basis of the project was to create a protocol where one could run useful data computations on sensitive data without actually seeing the data. As a result, we maintain privacy while producing a useful data analysis.
I worked specifically on the use cases of this new protocol. I used Twitter and Fitbit as the data providers. Twitter was an interesting use case because it was a live stream of data being provided. I focused solely on the location metadata because I wanted to demonstrate the dangers of Twitter’s public data. I used LDA to determine what topics in certain locations were being talked about.
Then, I applied a OPAL back end to show how Twitter could benefit from OPAL. Lastly, I created an algorithm to asses the health of Fitbit user by using their data. AI computations ran on this personal data and outputted a health score while maintaining the data private.

Gator IoT!!!

Internet of Things stole my heart two years ago. I loved the idea of machines talking to each other and to humans through a cloud. Data is power and IoT is a great medium in which we can see that. At University of Florida, there were no organizations or projects dedicated to IoT. Everyone spoke about the field, but no one was taking initiative to bring the field to UF’s students. Consequently, I created my own organization: Gator IoT. My organization’s mission was to promote IoT to all majors at UF through projects. We collaborated with the university, Marston Science Library, and multiple faculty on projects in order to expose students to the beauty of IoT through its ubiquity on campus.

Particle photon was our main sponsor. They provided us the funds and materials to prototype projects and host workshops to teach students to develop for IoT. We were also sponsored by Texas Instruments and Accenture. Below is some of our projects

Marston People Counter:

The pictures demonstrate the circuits involved in the project. We use Particle Photons as the main microcontroller, which is similar to an Arduino, but it has a cloud backend. We used two lasers that pointed at photoresistors across all the stairwells in the library and the main entrance. When someone broke the laser, we could determine if they were entering or leaving the library, so we could have an accurate count of people on each floor. The library’s administration had access to this valuable data in the form of an excel sheet to run computations on.

Marston Temperature:

A simple temperature reader of different areas around our Marston Science Library.

Sending a Message in Binary (Workshop):

I held a simple workshop to show students the potential and awesomeness of IoT. Also, I taught them how to code on the Particle Photon. I did this by creating a circuit where eight LEDs represented a 0 or 1 in binary. Then, I created a simple HTML view where one could enter a sentence or word and have it translated into binary. Once the user pressed entered, the Particle Photon would receive the message through the cloud. Thus, the message or word would aluminate the respective LED lights. We sent each other messages through this interface where we then had to decode the light sequence for the message. In theory, I could have had the Particle Photon in Australia and the Photon would have receive the message. This project was a cute way in which people could communicate long distances. Most importantly, it was a gateway for students to tinker some more with IoT.

Digital Currency Initiative

I was one of 25 chosen from 310 people to participate in DCI's weeklong cryptocurrency bootcamp. We spent a week at the Media Lab learning, in extreme detail, how cryptocurrency functioned. Not only did we learn the fundamentals, but we also learned about the cryptography and algorithms behind certain cryptocurrencies. Most importantly, we were taught how to develop for ethereum and other platforms. For example, we created simple smart contracts with ethereum, and we used 21 Inc. computers to run our own bitcoin scripts.

At the end of the bootcamp, me and a team created an email service where a user could send someone an email and then have those privileges removed. It was basically a snapchat for emails, but using the blockchain. Specifically, Dr. Guy Zyskind's Engima.

BCI Engagement

Using an Emotiv Insight for our BCI, we wanted to measure a user’s engagement in different activities such as studying or watching a TED talk. I developed an iPhone application that could do just that. We had users watch a TED talk to measure their engagement and then the results were given to them. This allowed them to keep track of their engagement over time and the opportunity to improve each time. As a result, we created a quantified-self application with a BCI as the medium. The code will be published once Dr. Andujar's dissertation is public.
